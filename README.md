<h2>Top ten most influential economists </h2>


<h3 class="athrsoc sticky_lft"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adam_Smith">Adam Smith&nbsp;</a></h3>

<h3>1723&ndash;1790&nbsp; &nbsp;</h3>
<p><strong>Adam Smith</strong>&nbsp;<span class="noexcerpt nowraplinks"><a title="Fellow of the Royal Society of Arts" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fellow_of_the_Royal_Society_of_Arts">FRSA</a></span>&nbsp;(<abbr title="circa">c.</abbr>&nbsp;16 June [<a title="Old Style and New Style dates" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Old_Style_and_New_Style_dates">O.S.</a>&nbsp;c. 5 June] 1723<sup id="cite_ref-baptism_1-1" class="reference"></sup>&nbsp;&ndash; 17 July 1790) was a Scottish&nbsp;<a title="Economist" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economist">economist</a>,&nbsp;<a title="Philosopher" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philosopher">philosopher</a>, and&nbsp;<a title="Author" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Author">author</a>&nbsp;as well as a&nbsp;<a title="Ethics" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethics">moral philosopher</a>, a pioneer of&nbsp;<a title="Political economy" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_economy">political economy</a>, and a key figure during the&nbsp;<a title="Scottish Enlightenment" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scottish_Enlightenment">Scottish Enlightenment</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"></sup>&nbsp;also known as ''The Father of Economics''<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"></sup>&nbsp;or ''The Father of Capitalism''.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"></sup>&nbsp;Smith wrote two classic works,&nbsp;<em><a title="The Theory of Moral Sentiments" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Theory_of_Moral_Sentiments">The Theory of Moral Sentiments</a></em>&nbsp;(1759) and&nbsp;<em><a title="The Wealth of Nations" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Wealth_of_Nations">An Inquiry into the Nature and Causes of the Wealth of Nations</a></em>&nbsp;(1776). The latter, often abbreviated as&nbsp;<em>The Wealth of Nations</em>, is considered his&nbsp;<em><a title="Masterpiece" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Masterpiece">magnum opus</a></em>&nbsp;and the first modern work of economics. In his work, Adam Smith introduced his theory of&nbsp;<a title="Absolute advantage" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_advantage">absolute advantage</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"></sup></p>
<p>Smith studied&nbsp;<a title="Social philosophy" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_philosophy">social philosophy</a>&nbsp;at the&nbsp;<a title="University of Glasgow" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Glasgow">University of Glasgow</a>&nbsp;and at&nbsp;<a title="Balliol College, Oxford" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Balliol_College,_Oxford">Balliol College</a>,&nbsp;<a title="University of Oxford" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Oxford">Oxford</a>, where he was one of the first students to benefit from scholarships set up by fellow Scot&nbsp;<a title="John Snell" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Snell">John Snell</a>. After graduating, he delivered a successful series of public lectures at the&nbsp;<a title="University of Edinburgh" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Edinburgh">University of Edinburgh</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"></sup>&nbsp;leading him to collaborate with&nbsp;<a title="David Hume" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/David_Hume">David Hume</a>&nbsp;during the Scottish Enlightenment. Smith obtained a professorship at Glasgow, teaching moral philosophy and during this time, wrote and published&nbsp;<em>The Theory of Moral Sentiments</em>. In his later life, he took a tutoring position that allowed him to travel throughout Europe, where he met other intellectual leaders of his day.</p>
<p>Smith laid the foundations of classical&nbsp;<a title="Free market" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_market">free market</a>&nbsp;economic theory.&nbsp;<em>The Wealth of Nations</em>&nbsp;was a precursor to the modern academic discipline of economics. In this and other works, he developed the concept of&nbsp;<a title="Division of labour" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Division_of_labour#Adam_Smith">division of labour</a>&nbsp;and expounded upon how rational self-interest and competition can lead to economic prosperity. Smith was controversial in his own day and his general approach and writing style were often satirised by writers such as&nbsp;<a title="Horace Walpole" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Horace_Walpole">Horace Walpole</a>.</p>

<h3><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alfred_Marshall">Alfred Marshall&nbsp;</a></h3>
<h3>1842&ndash;1924</h3>
<p><strong>Alfred Marshall</strong>&nbsp;<span class="noexcerpt nowraplinks"><a title="Fellow of the British Academy" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fellow_of_the_British_Academy">FBA</a></span>&nbsp;(26 July 1842 &ndash; 13 July 1924) was one of the most influential&nbsp;<a title="Economist" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economist">economists</a>&nbsp;of his time. His book,&nbsp;<em><a title="Principles of Economics (Marshall)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Principles_of_Economics_(Marshall)">Principles of Economics</a></em>&nbsp;(1890), was the dominant economic textbook in England for many years. It brings the ideas of&nbsp;<a title="Supply and demand" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supply_and_demand">supply and demand</a>,&nbsp;<a title="Marginal utility" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marginal_utility">marginal utility</a>, and&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Costs of production" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Costs_of_production">costs of production</a>&nbsp;into a coherent whole. He is known as one of the founders of&nbsp;<a title="Neoclassical economics" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neoclassical_economics">neoclassical economics</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"></sup>&nbsp;Although Marshall took&nbsp;<a title="Economics" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economics">economics</a>&nbsp;to a more mathematically rigorous level, he did not want mathematics to overshadow economics and thus make economics irrelevant to the layman.</p>

<h3><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Millicent_Fawcett">Millicent Fawcett&nbsp;</a></h3>
<h3>1847&ndash;1929</h3>
<p><strong>Dame Millicent Garrett Fawcett</strong>&nbsp;<span class="noexcerpt nowraplinks"><a class="mw-redirect" title="Knight Grand Cross of the Order of the British Empire" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knight_Grand_Cross_of_the_Order_of_the_British_Empire">GBE</a></span>&nbsp;(11 June 1847 &ndash; 5 August 1929) was an English political leader, writer and feminist icon. Known for campaigning for&nbsp;<a title="Women's suffrage in the United Kingdom" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Women%27s_suffrage_in_the_United_Kingdom">women's suffrage</a>&nbsp;through&nbsp;<a title="Law reform" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Law_reform">legislative change</a>, she led Britain's largest women's rights organisation, the&nbsp;<a title="National Union of Women's Suffrage Societies" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Union_of_Women%27s_Suffrage_Societies">National Union of Women's Suffrage Societies</a>&nbsp;(NUWSS) from 1897 until 1919.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"></sup>&nbsp;She would write: "I cannot say I became a suffragist. I always was one, from the time I was old enough to think at all about the principles of Representative Government."<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"></sup>&nbsp;Fawcett also tried to improve women's chances of higher education, serving as a governor of&nbsp;<a title="Bedford College, London" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bedford_College,_London">Bedford College, London</a>&nbsp;(now&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Royal Holloway" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Royal_Holloway">Royal Holloway</a>), and a co-founder of&nbsp;<a title="Newnham College, Cambridge" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newnham_College,_Cambridge">Newnham College, Cambridge</a>, in 1875.<sup id="cite_ref-odnb_3-0" class="reference"></sup>&nbsp;In 2018, 100 years after the passing of the&nbsp;<a title="Representation of the People Act 1918" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Representation_of_the_People_Act_1918">Representation of the People Act</a>, Fawcett became the first woman to be commemorated with&nbsp;<a title="Statue of Millicent Fawcett" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statue_of_Millicent_Fawcett">a statue in Parliament Square</a>.</p>

<h3><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Maynard_Keynes">John Maynard Keynes&nbsp;</a></h3>
<h3>1883&ndash;1946</h3>
<p><strong>John Maynard Keynes, 1st Baron Keynes</strong><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"></sup>&nbsp;<span class="noexcerpt nowraplinks"><a class="mw-redirect" title="Companion of the Order of the Bath" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Companion_of_the_Order_of_the_Bath">CB</a>&nbsp;<a title="Fellow of the British Academy" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fellow_of_the_British_Academy">FBA</a></span>&nbsp;(<span class="rt-commentedText nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a title="Help:IPA/English" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:IPA/English">/<span title="'k' in 'kind'">k</span><span title="/eɪ/: 'a' in 'face'">eɪ</span><span title="'n' in 'nigh'">n</span><span title="'z' in 'zoom'">z</span>/</a></span></span>&nbsp;<a title="Help:Pronunciation respelling key" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key"><em title="English pronunciation respelling">KAYNZ</em></a>; 5 June 1883 &ndash; 21 April 1946), was a British&nbsp;<a title="Economist" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economist">economist</a>, whose ideas fundamentally changed the theory and practice of&nbsp;<a title="Macroeconomics" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Macroeconomics">macroeconomics</a>&nbsp;and the&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Economic policies" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economic_policies">economic policies</a>&nbsp;of governments. Originally trained in mathematics, he built on and greatly refined earlier work on the causes of&nbsp;<a title="Business cycle" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Business_cycle">business cycles</a>, and was one of the most influential economists of the 20th century.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEYerginStanislaw200239&ndash;42_3-0" class="reference"></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"></sup>&nbsp;Widely considered the founder of modern&nbsp;<a title="Macroeconomics" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Macroeconomics">macroeconomics</a>, his ideas are the basis for the&nbsp;<a title="Schools of economic thought" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schools_of_economic_thought">school of thought</a>&nbsp;known as&nbsp;<a title="Keynesian economics" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Keynesian_economics">Keynesian economics</a>, and its various offshoots.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"></sup></p>
<p>During the&nbsp;<a title="Great Depression" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_Depression">Great Depression</a>&nbsp;of the 1930s, Keynes spearheaded&nbsp;<a title="Keynesian Revolution" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Keynesian_Revolution">a revolution in economic thinking</a>, challenging the ideas of&nbsp;<a title="Neoclassical economics" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neoclassical_economics">neoclassical economics</a>&nbsp;that held that&nbsp;<a title="Free market" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_market">free markets</a>&nbsp;would, in the short to medium term, automatically provide full employment, as long as workers were flexible in their wage demands. He argued that&nbsp;<a title="Aggregate demand" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aggregate_demand">aggregate demand</a>&nbsp;(total spending in the economy) determined the overall level of economic activity, and that inadequate aggregate demand could lead to prolonged periods of high&nbsp;<a title="Unemployment" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unemployment">unemployment</a>. Keynes advocated the use of&nbsp;<a title="Fiscal policy" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fiscal_policy">fiscal</a>&nbsp;and&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Monetary policies" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monetary_policies">monetary policies</a>&nbsp;to mitigate the adverse effects of economic&nbsp;<a title="Recession" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Recession">recessions</a>&nbsp;and&nbsp;<a title="Depression (economics)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Depression_(economics)">depressions</a>. He detailed these ideas in his magnum opus,&nbsp;<em><a title="The General Theory of Employment, Interest and Money" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_General_Theory_of_Employment,_Interest_and_Money">The General Theory of Employment, Interest and Money</a></em>, published in 1936. By the late 1930s, leading Western economies had begun adopting Keynes's policy recommendations. Almost all&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Capitalist" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Capitalist">capitalist</a>&nbsp;governments had done so by the end of the two decades following Keynes's death in 1946. As a leader of the British delegation, Keynes participated in the design of the international economic institutions established after the end of&nbsp;<a title="World War II" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_II">World War II</a>&nbsp;but was overruled by the American delegation on several aspects.</p>
<p>Keynes's influence started to wane in the 1970s, partly as a result of the&nbsp;<a title="Stagflation" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stagflation">stagflation</a>&nbsp;that plagued the&nbsp;<a title="Anglo-America" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anglo-America">Anglo-American</a>&nbsp;economies during that decade, and partly because of criticism of Keynesian policies by&nbsp;<a title="Milton Friedman" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Milton_Friedman">Milton Friedman</a>&nbsp;and other&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Monetarists" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monetarists">monetarists</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"></sup>&nbsp;who disputed the ability of government to favourably regulate the business cycle with&nbsp;<a title="Fiscal policy" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fiscal_policy">fiscal policy</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"></sup>&nbsp;However, the advent of the global&nbsp;<a title="Financial crisis of 2007&ndash;2008" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Financial_crisis_of_2007%E2%80%932008">financial crisis of 2007&ndash;2008</a>&nbsp;sparked a&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="2008&ndash;09 Keynesian resurgence" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2008%E2%80%9309_Keynesian_resurgence">resurgence in Keynesian thought</a>. Keynesian economics provided the theoretical underpinning for economic policies undertaken in response to the financial crisis of 2007&ndash;2008 by President&nbsp;<a title="Barack Obama" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barack_Obama">Barack Obama</a>&nbsp;of the United States, Prime Minister&nbsp;<a title="Gordon Brown" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gordon_Brown">Gordon Brown</a>&nbsp;of the United Kingdom, and other heads of governments.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"></sup></p>
<p>When&nbsp;<em><a title="Time (magazine)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time_(magazine)">Time</a></em>&nbsp;magazine included Keynes among its&nbsp;<a title="Time 100: The Most Important People of the Century" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time_100:_The_Most_Important_People_of_the_Century">Most Important People of the Century</a>&nbsp;in 1999, it stated that "his radical idea that governments should spend money they don't have may have saved capitalism."<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"></sup>&nbsp;<em><a title="The Economist" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Economist">The Economist</a></em>&nbsp;has described Keynes as "Britain's most famous 20th-century economist."<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"></sup>&nbsp;In addition to being an economist, Keynes was also a civil servant, a director of the&nbsp;<a title="Bank of England" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bank_of_England">Bank of England</a>, and a part of the&nbsp;<a title="Bloomsbury Group" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bloomsbury_Group">Bloomsbury Group</a>&nbsp;of intellectuals.</p>

<h3><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Milton_Friedman">Milton Friedman&nbsp;</a></h3>
<h3>1912&ndash;2006</h3>
<p><strong>Milton Friedman</strong>&nbsp;(<span class="rt-commentedText nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a title="Help:IPA/English" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:IPA/English">/<span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'f' in 'find'">f</span><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/iː/: 'ee' in 'fleece'">iː</span><span title="'d' in 'dye'">d</span><span title="'m' in 'my'">m</span><span title="/ən/: 'on' in 'button'">ən</span>/</a></span></span>; July 31, 1912 &ndash; November 16, 2006) was an American&nbsp;<a title="Economist" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economist">economist</a>&nbsp;who received the 1976&nbsp;<a title="Nobel Memorial Prize in Economic Sciences" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nobel_Memorial_Prize_in_Economic_Sciences">Nobel Memorial Prize in Economic Sciences</a>&nbsp;for his research on&nbsp;<a title="Consumption (economics)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Consumption_(economics)">consumption</a>&nbsp;analysis,&nbsp;<a title="Money supply" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Money_supply">monetary</a>&nbsp;history and theory and the complexity of&nbsp;<a title="Stabilization policy" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stabilization_policy">stabilization policy</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-nobel1_4-0" class="reference"></sup>&nbsp;With&nbsp;<a title="George Stigler" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/George_Stigler">George Stigler</a>&nbsp;and others, Friedman was among the intellectual leaders of the&nbsp;<a title="Chicago school of economics" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chicago_school_of_economics">Chicago school of economics</a>, a&nbsp;<a title="Neoclassical economics" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neoclassical_economics">neoclassical</a>&nbsp;school of economic thought associated with the work of the faculty at the&nbsp;<a title="University of Chicago" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Chicago">University of Chicago</a>&nbsp;that rejected&nbsp;<a title="Keynesian economics" mid-1970s, when it turned to&nbsp;<a title="New classical macroeconomics" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_classical_macroeconomics">new classical macroeconomics</a>&nbsp;heavily based on the concept of&nbsp;<a title="Rational expectations" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rational_expectations">rational expectations</a>. Several students and young professors who were recruited or mentored by Friedman at Chicago went on to become leading economists, including&nbsp;<a title="Gary Becker" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gary_Becker">Gary Becker</a>,&nbsp;<a title="Robert Fogel" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Fogel">Robert Fogel</a>,&nbsp;<a title="Thomas Sowell" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thomas_Sowell">Thomas Sowell</a><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"></sup>&nbsp;and&nbsp;<a title="Robert Lucas Jr." href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Lucas_Jr.">Robert Lucas Jr.</a><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"></sup></p>
<p>Friedman's challenges to what he later called "naive&nbsp;<a title="Keynesian economics" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Keynesian_economics">Keynesian</a>" theory<sup id="cite_ref-pbsinterview_7-0" class="reference"></sup>&nbsp;began with his 1950s reinterpretation of the&nbsp;<a title="Consumption function" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Consumption_function">consumption function</a>. In the 1960s, he became the main advocate opposing Keynesian government policies<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"></sup>&nbsp;and described his approach (along with mainstream economics) as using "Keynesian language and apparatus" yet rejecting its "initial" conclusions.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"></sup>&nbsp;He theorized that there existed a&nbsp;<a title="Natural rate of unemployment" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_rate_of_unemployment">"natural" rate of unemployment</a>&nbsp;and argued that unemployment below this rate would cause inflation to accelerate.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"></sup>&nbsp;He argued that the&nbsp;<a title="Phillips curve" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phillips_curve">Phillips curve</a>&nbsp;was in the long run vertical at the "natural rate" and predicted what would come to be known as&nbsp;<a title="Stagflation" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stagflation">stagflation</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"></sup>&nbsp;Friedman promoted an alternative macroeconomic viewpoint known as "<a title="Monetarism" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monetarism">monetarism</a>" and argued that a steady, small expansion of the&nbsp;<a title="Money supply" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Money_supply">money supply</a>&nbsp;was the preferred policy.<sup id="cite_ref-bestofbothworlds_12-0" class="reference"></sup>&nbsp;His ideas concerning&nbsp;<a title="Monetary policy" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monetary_policy">monetary policy</a>,&nbsp;<a title="Tax" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tax">taxation</a>,&nbsp;<a title="Privatization" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Privatization">privatization</a>&nbsp;and&nbsp;<a title="Deregulation" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deregulation">deregulation</a>&nbsp;influenced government policies, especially during the 1980s. His&nbsp;<a title="Monetarism" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monetarism">monetary theory</a>&nbsp;influenced the Federal Reserve's response to the&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Financial crisis of 2007&ndash;08" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Financial_crisis_of_2007%E2%80%9308">global financial crisis of 2007&ndash;2008</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"></sup></p>
<p>Friedman was an advisor to&nbsp;<a title="Republican Party (United States)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Republican_Party_(United_States)">Republican</a>&nbsp;President&nbsp;<a title="Ronald Reagan" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ronald_Reagan">Ronald Reagan</a><sup id="cite_ref-Ebenstein_2007_208_3-1" class="reference"></sup>&nbsp;and&nbsp;<a title="Conservative Party (UK)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conservative_Party_(UK)">Conservative</a>&nbsp;British Prime Minister&nbsp;<a title="Margaret Thatcher" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Margaret_Thatcher">Margaret Thatcher</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-google576_2-1" class="reference"></sup>&nbsp;His political philosophy extolled the virtues of a&nbsp;<a title="Free market" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_market">free market</a>&nbsp;economic system with minimal intervention. He once stated that his role in eliminating&nbsp;<a title="Conscription in the United States" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conscription_in_the_United_States">conscription in the United States</a>&nbsp;was his proudest accomplishment. In his 1962 book&nbsp;<em><a title="Capitalism and Freedom" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Capitalism_and_Freedom">Capitalism and Freedom</a></em>, Friedman advocated policies such as a&nbsp;<a title="Volunteer military" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volunteer_military">volunteer military</a>, freely&nbsp;<a title="Floating exchange rate" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Floating_exchange_rate">floating exchange rates</a>, abolition of&nbsp;<a title="Medical license" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medical_license">medical licenses</a>, a&nbsp;<a title="Negative income tax" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Negative_income_tax">negative income tax</a>&nbsp;and&nbsp;<a title="School voucher" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/School_voucher">school vouchers</a><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"></sup>&nbsp;and opposed the&nbsp;<a title="War on drugs" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/War_on_drugs">war on drugs</a>. His support for&nbsp;<a title="School choice" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/School_choice">school choice</a>&nbsp;led him to found the Friedman Foundation for Educational Choice, later renamed&nbsp;<a title="EdChoice" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/EdChoice">EdChoice</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-sullivan_15-0" class="reference"></sup></p>
<p><a title="Milton Friedman bibliography" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Milton_Friedman_bibliography">Friedman's works</a>&nbsp;include monographs, books, scholarly articles, papers, magazine columns, television programs, and lectures, and cover a broad range of economic topics and public policy issues.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"></sup>&nbsp;His books and essays have had global influence, including in former&nbsp;<a title="Communist state" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Communist_state">communist states</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"></sup><sup id="cite_ref-defaming_19-0" class="reference"></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"></sup>&nbsp;A survey of economists ranked Friedman as the second-most popular economist of the 20th century, following only&nbsp;<a title="John Maynard Keynes" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Maynard_Keynes">John Maynard Keynes</a><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"></sup>, and&nbsp;<em><a title="The Economist" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Economist">The Economist</a></em>&nbsp;described him as "the most influential economist of the second half of the 20th century ... possibly of all of it".</p>

<h3><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/W._Arthur_Lewis">W. Arthur Lewis&nbsp;</a></h3>
<h3>1915&ndash;1991</h3>
<p><strong>Sir William Arthur Lewis</strong>&nbsp;(23 January 1915 &ndash; 15 June 1991) was an&nbsp;<a title="Economist" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economist">economist</a>&nbsp;and the James Madison Professor of Political Economy at&nbsp;<a title="Princeton University" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Princeton_University">Princeton University</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"></sup>&nbsp;Lewis was well known for his contributions in the field of&nbsp;<a title="Economic development" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economic_development">economic development</a>. In 1979 he was awarded the&nbsp;<a title="Nobel Memorial Prize in Economic Sciences" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nobel_Memorial_Prize_in_Economic_Sciences">Nobel Memorial Prize in Economic Sciences</a>. He had dual&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Saint Lucian people" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saint_Lucian_people">Saint Lucian</a>&nbsp;and&nbsp;<a title="United Kingdom" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Kingdom">British</a>&nbsp;citizenships.</p>

<h3><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Warren_Buffett">Warren Buffett&nbsp;</a></h3>
<h3>1930</h3>
<p><strong>Warren Edward Buffett</strong>&nbsp;(<span class="rt-commentedText nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a title="Help:IPA/English" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:IPA/English">/<span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'b' in 'buy'">b</span><span title="/ʌ/: 'u' in 'cut'">ʌ</span><span title="'f' in 'find'">f</span><span title="/ɪ/: 'i' in 'kit'">ɪ</span><span title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span>/</a></span></span>; born August 30, 1930)<sup id="cite_ref-biography.com_2-0" class="reference"></sup>&nbsp;is an American investor, business tycoon, and philanthropist, who is the chairman and CEO of&nbsp;<a title="Berkshire Hathaway" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Berkshire_Hathaway">Berkshire Hathaway</a>. He is considered one of the most successful investors in the world<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"></sup>&nbsp;and has a&nbsp;<a title="Net worth" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Net_worth">net worth</a>&nbsp;of&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="US$" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/US$">US$</a>88.9&nbsp;billion as of December 2019, making him the&nbsp;<a title="The World's Billionaires" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_World%27s_Billionaires">fourth-wealthiest person in the world</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"></sup></p>
<p>Buffett was born in&nbsp;<a title="Omaha, Nebraska" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Omaha,_Nebraska">Omaha</a>,&nbsp;<a title="Nebraska" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nebraska">Nebraska</a>. He developed an interest in business and investing in his youth, eventually entering the&nbsp;<a title="Wharton School of the University of Pennsylvania" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wharton_School_of_the_University_of_Pennsylvania">Wharton School of the University of Pennsylvania</a>&nbsp;in 1947 before transferring and graduating from the&nbsp;<a title="University of Nebraska&ndash;Lincoln" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Nebraska%E2%80%93Lincoln">University of Nebraska</a>&nbsp;at the age of 19. He went on to graduate from&nbsp;<a title="Columbia Business School" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Columbia_Business_School">Columbia Business School</a>, where he molded his investment philosophy around the concept of&nbsp;<a title="Value investing" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Value_investing">value investing</a>&nbsp;that was pioneered by&nbsp;<a title="Benjamin Graham" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benjamin_Graham">Benjamin Graham</a>. He attended&nbsp;<a title="New York Institute of Finance" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_York_Institute_of_Finance">New York Institute of Finance</a>&nbsp;to focus his economics background and soon after began various business partnerships, including one with Graham. He created Buffett Partnership, Ltd in 1956 and his firm eventually acquired a textile manufacturing firm called&nbsp;<a title="Berkshire Hathaway" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Berkshire_Hathaway">Berkshire Hathaway</a>, assuming its name to create a diversified holding company. In 1978, Charlie Munger joined Buffett and became vice-chairman of the company.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"></sup></p>
<p>Buffett has been the chairman and largest shareholder of Berkshire Hathaway since 1970.<sup id="cite_ref-investopedia_8-0" class="reference"></sup>&nbsp;He has been referred to as the "Oracle" or "Sage" of&nbsp;<a title="Omaha, Nebraska" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Omaha,_Nebraska">Omaha</a>&nbsp;by global media outlets.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"></sup>&nbsp;He is noted for his adherence to value investing and for his personal frugality despite his immense wealth.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"></sup>&nbsp;Research published at the&nbsp;<a title="University of Oxford" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Oxford">University of Oxford</a>&nbsp;characterizes Buffett's investment methodology as falling within "founder centrism" &ndash; defined by a deference to managers with a founder's mindset, an ethical disposition towards the shareholder collective, and an intense focus on exponential value creation. Essentially, Buffett's concentrated investments shelter managers from the short-term pressures of the market.<sup id="cite_ref-:5_12-0" class="reference"></sup></p>
<p>Buffett is a notable philanthropist, having pledged to give away 99 percent<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"></sup>&nbsp;of his fortune to philanthropic causes, primarily via the&nbsp;<a title="" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bill_%26_Melinda_Gates_Foundation">Bill &amp; Melinda Gates Foundation</a>. He founded&nbsp;<a title="The Giving Pledge" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Giving_Pledge">The Giving Pledge</a>&nbsp;in 2009 with&nbsp;<a title="Bill Gates" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bill_Gates">Bill Gates</a>, whereby billionaires pledge to give away at least half of their fortunes.</p>
